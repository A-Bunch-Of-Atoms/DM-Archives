When new players say "night first" I am assuming that you are used to playing with investigating town roles which get some information before the game even begins, yes? This means that you are used to very mechanical games which are heavily dictated by relying on the information given to you by the few players who have a role to obtain said information. Games like this usually revolve around these roles. There isn't really anything wrong with that except that there is in regards to gameplay layers involved, since this in turn means that social reading is going to take a big step back. This isn't because social reading is useless, but because communities that heavily rely on mechanical information usually don't have a need to get good at this. They play the game differently than you'd do if you needed to rely on social reads to get your information on other players making it an underdeveloped skill. Calling it "inferior" is imo an injustice, because imo players who have an underdeveloped social reading skill are the ones who are actually playing the inferior version, at least in terms of playing mafia, because in its purest form, there are no mechanical roles at all in the first place. Just vanilla town and vanilla mafia. The original rulebook even intended for there to not be any night phases at all, unless the town called for a night by a majority vote. You can read up on it if it interests you! :D Again, nothing wrong with liking modern mechanical games, but its definitely not inferior over the other! Calling it such is honestly just skill issue B) as it shows that you have trouble with the social aspect of mafia (in the sense, that you find it difficult to extract alignment indicative information from conversations with other players)! Learning this takes quite a lot of time, although there are some awesome guides out there that can definitely help you improve a lot quicker. :D

5:32 p.m.
-- Which leads me to the next thing I wanted to address!
Confirmed information >>>>> human opinions (like reads)
As we just discussed while we looked at Mel's article about no-lynching day 1, purely relying on power roles to solve the game for you is not a good idea. And you, as a member of the town, irregardless of your role, citizen or not, should always try your best to create and extract information. You do not need a night action to do this! Guessing, speculating, threatening, voting, analysing, you can do many things to facilitate the creation of new information with your voice and vote alone! It takes some amount of instinct, ingenuity and dedication, but it is very possible to come to the right conclusions without a night phase ever occuring. Sometimes you are going to be right, sometimes you are going to be wrong, sometimes you are right, but for all the wrong reasons. That's part of the game and embracing it is inherently going to make it more enjoyable, because it will lead you to interact with the other players, exchange your thoughts, come up with ideas and make decisions together. And you don't need to be talking about the cop's night check that he claimed to do that. You can discuss people's votes, their attitudes, their ways of thinking, their opinions of other players! There's lots of things that you can talk about in a game of mafia. Many of these things won't end up mattering, but they might, and discussing them is always going to have better results than not doing so, because even if you are wrong, you then know that you ARE wrong and that automatically eliminates a lot of things that have been thought about to that point, which will inevitably lead to the remaining thoughts to be more accurate than they were previously. This starts with day one and ends once the game is over, every day the town can make an effort to come up with ideas and eliminate them if they seem unlikely. Its a big think tank that is ever evolving and moves from speculation to speculation until it finds something that fits, seemingly, or in factuality. This process is why human opinions matter, even if they don't discuss objective information.
